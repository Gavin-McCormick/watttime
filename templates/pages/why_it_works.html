Why it Works


Techniques in Action Today

Using WattTime, electricity consumers can optimize their electricity use to promote the development of cleaner energy sources while lowering greenhouse gas emissions.

Percent Clean Energy

WattTime shows people how much of their region's electricity comes from clean and renewable sources. This can help shift demand towards sustainable fuel sources.

Marginal Fuel

With the ability to see current marginal fuel sources in their region, electricity consumers can shift their demand as they like towards more renewable fuels such as hydropower and biomass away from dirty sources such as coal and oil. (Currently implemented in New England only; additional regions coming soon).

Coming soon

By combining open data with Renewables Integration Response analytics, WattTime is working to equip anyone who wants them with powerful, real-time economic instruments that increase renewable power competitiveness and reduce system emissions without reducing power availability. (These are currently under development by our crack team of economists and data analysts from UC Berkeley and elsewhere.)

Reducing Curtailment

By taking advantage of periods of wind power oversupply, electricity customers in select locations will be able to reduce indirect emissions by up to 100% at optimal times.

Renewables Intermittency

Device automation allows for fine-grained energy response timing. It can leverage minor spikes in the availability of renewable energy to help make renewable energy more competitive with fossil fuel-powered plants like coal.

Reducing Ramping

With automation, users can even reduce pollution from traditional fossil power plants by allowing them to reduce wasteful episodes of turning up and down repeatedly.

Forecast Insurance

Energy experts know that high-quality forecasts can help integrate renewable energy and let the most efficient plants operate. But what if instead of just improving forecasts, you could help them come true? WattTime monitors the grid for situations where shifting load to match the forecast would help grid operators select cleaner power plants.
